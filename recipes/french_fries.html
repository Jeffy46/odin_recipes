French Fries

French fries, often simply referred to as fries, are a beloved snack and side dish that have become a staple in cuisines around the world. Typically made from potatoes, they are cut into thin, elongated shapes and then fried to crispy perfection. The process begins with peeling and cutting the potatoes into strips, which can vary in thickness depending on the desired style—ranging from shoestring to steak fries. These potato slices are then submerged in hot oil until they turn golden brown and crispy on the outside, while remaining soft and fluffy on the inside. The fries are usually seasoned with a pinch of salt right after frying, which enhances their flavor and adds to the satisfying crunch with every bite.
French fries are incredibly versatile and can be enjoyed in numerous ways. They are often served as a classic side dish to burgers, sandwiches, and various main courses, but they also shine as a stand-alone snack. In addition to the traditional ketchup, fries can be paired with an array of dipping sauces, such as mayonnaise, aioli, cheese sauce, or gravy. Some variations include loaded fries, topped with ingredients like melted cheese, bacon bits, and jalapeños, or poutine, a Canadian specialty featuring fries smothered in gravy and cheese curds. Whether savored in fast-food joints, upscale restaurants, or homemade in kitchens, French fries offer a comforting and universally loved experience that transcends culinary boundaries.


Ingredients

  • Potatoes
  • Oil

  1. Heat oil to 450
  2. Gently throw in until golden brown
  3. Let dry on paper towel and lightly sprinkle with salt
